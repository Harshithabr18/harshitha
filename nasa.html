GENDER AND CLIMATE

The United Nations Sustainable Development Goals are a series of targets designed to promote human development and protect the environment. Two of the goals concern gender equality and climate action. While most people might consider these two topics to be quite disparate, they are actually closely related. Your challenge is to model the relationship between climate change and gender inequality, and propose a solution that promotes equality and action to support sustainable development for all.

ALLIANCE OF GENDER AND CLIMATE CHANGE:

In African pastoralist societies, particularly in countries like Kenya, Ethiopia, and Somalia, the gendered division of labor plays a crucial role in how communities manage their livelihoods. Pastoralist livelihoods are highly dependent on livestock rearing, which in turn is deeply influenced by access to natural resources like water and grazing land. Climate change, particularly droughts, has a significant impact on this gendered division of labor, exacerbating gender inequalities.

Intersectionality of Climate and Gender Inequality:

  1. Women's livelihoods more vulnerable to climate-related shocks.
  2. Climate-related migration increases women's care work.
  3. Limited access to climate information and adaptation resources.
  4. Increased risk of gender-based violence during climate-related displacement.
  5. Exclusion from climate decision-making processes.

IMPACTS ON PASTORALISTS DUE TO PARTRIACHAL SOCIETY:

1. Gendered Division of Labor in Pastoralist Societies.

In traditional pastoralist societies, roles and responsibilities are divided along gender lines: Men's roles: Typically, men are responsible for herding and managing large livestock like cattle, camels, or goats, which often requires traveling long distances in search of pasture and water. They are also involved in negotiating land use and conducting trade. Women's roles: Women, on the other hand, are largely tasked with household duties, including fetching water, gathering firewood, preparing food, caring for children, and managing small livestock like poultry. They are also responsible for milking animals and processing dairy products, which are essential to the household's nutritional and economic well-being. This division of labor has traditionally supported pastoralist communities' survival in harsh environments, but climate change, particularly droughts, is disrupting this balance.

2.Impact of Climate-Change-Driven Droughts

a. Increased Workload for Women:

Water scarcity:

Droughts intensify water scarcity, which increases the time and distance women must travel to find water for their families and livestock. This adds to their already heavy workload, leaving them less time for other economic activities, education, or social engagement.

Foraging for resources:

As grazing lands shrink and become less fertile, women are often forced to forage for alternative sources of food, firewood, and animal fodder. This further burdens them with physically demanding tasks and increases their exposure to health risks and insecurity.

Reduced food production:

The decline in livestock productivity due to droughts, especially the death of small livestock, undermines women's ability to contribute to household food security. Women often manage milk production, which decreases during droughts, affecting both nutrition and income generation for the family.

b.Male Migration and Shifts in Labor:

Men's migration:

In many cases, men migrate in search of greener pastures or alternative sources of income, such as urban or semi-urban labor. This migration leaves women behind to manage the household, livestock, and other community responsibilities, effectively increasing their labor burden and shifting traditional gender roles.

Shifts in decision-making power:

With the increased responsibilities that come with male migration, women may find themselves making decisions that were traditionally in the male domain, such as livestock management and negotiating land access. However, this shift in responsibility does not necessarily translate into increased decision-making power or autonomy, as patriarchal norms often limit women's control over resources.

3.Socio-Economic and Health Impacts on Women

a.Economic Disempowerment

Loss of livelihood assets:

Droughts lead to livestock deaths, which directly impacts women's income from dairy products and small livestock sales. As livestock die or are sold off cheaply to cope with food shortages, women's ability to sustain themselves economically diminishes, exacerbating poverty and dependence on external aid.

Limited access to credit and resources:

Women, who already have less access to credit, land, and resources than men, struggle to recover from drought-induced losses. With fewer livestock, they are less able to access loans or rebuild their herds.

b. Health Implications

Malnutrition:

The reduction in milk production during droughts affects children's nutrition, especially since pastoralist diets heavily rely on dairy. Women, responsible for managing household nutrition, bear the stress of coping with food shortages, often eating less themselves to feed their children.

Increased vulnerability to gender-based violence:

As water and food scarcity intensifies, women may be exposed to greater risks of violence during their search for resources. This can include sexual harassment or assault during long treks to water points or while negotiating for scarce supplies in hostile or competitive environments.

4.Cultural and Social Implications

Erosion of Traditional Roles

Disruption of traditional roles:

With men migrating for work or searching for pasture, the traditional gender roles within pastoralist communities are disrupted. While this can lead to temporary empowerment of women as decision-makers, the social stress it creates within patriarchal systems often reinforces conservative gender norms in the long run.

Increased conflict and competition:

Droughts also intensify competition over dwindling resources, leading to conflict between different pastoralist groups or between pastoralists and other land users, such as farmers. Women and children often suffer the most in such conflicts, being displaced or losing access to critical resources.

5.Long-Term Impacts on Gender Relations

Deepening Gender Inequality

Marginalization in adaptation strategies:

Despite their central role in managing household resources and coping with climate impacts, women are often excluded from formal climate adaptation and decision-making processes. Development projects or interventions may focus on livestock and land, typically managed by men, sidelining women's critical role in managing household resources.

Entrenched patriarchy:

While climate change is shifting the division of labor, traditional patriarchal systems are often reinforced in response to social stress, with men reasserting control over resources and decision-making power. This undermines any potential for long-term empowerment of women that could arise from their increased responsibilities during crises.

ADAPTIVE STRATEGIES

Pastoralists, who rely heavily on livestock for their livelihood, employ various adaptive strategies to cope with environmental, social, and economic challenges. Mobility is a key strategy, with pastoralists often practicing transhumance or nomadism, moving their herds seasonally to access optimal grazing areas and water sources. They also diversify their livelihoods by engaging in crop farming, trade, or wage labor, and by keeping a mix of livestock species that can thrive in different conditions. Social networks and reciprocity are essential, as pastoralists rely on family and clan networks for support during crises, sharing resources such as water and grazing land. Effective rangeland management, including controlled grazing and collective management of resources, helps prevent overgrazing and degradation. Many pastoralists also adopt new technologies, such as improved veterinary practices and water conservation techniques, to enhance livestock health and secure water during droughts. To adapt to climate change, they may shift to drought-resistant livestock and use early warning systems to better plan for adverse weather conditions. Market engagement is another strategy, where pastoralists sell livestock or products at favorable prices and form agreements to secure demand. Additionally, they advocate for land and resource rights through engagement with governments and community-based organizations. Finally, pastoralists manage risk through mechanisms like livestock insurance and cultural practices that emphasize resource-sharing during crises. 6.Policy and Advocacy

1. National policies supporting pastoralism.

National policies supporting pastoralism are critical for promoting sustainable livelihoods, economic resilience, and the preservation of the cultural heritage of pastoralist communities. Pastoralism, which involves the raising and herding of livestock in extensive rangelands, plays a crucial role in many countries’ economies, food security, and environmental stewardship. Effective policies recognize these contributions while addressing the unique challenges faced by pastoralists, such as mobility, land tenure, climate change, and access to services.

2. International advocacy for pastoralist rights.

International advocacy for pastoralist rights has gained significant traction in recent years, with numerous organizations, networks, and global forums working to ensure the recognition and protection of pastoralist livelihoods, culture, and land rights. Given the unique challenges faced by pastoralist communities—such as marginalization, climate change impacts, resource conflicts, and lack of political representation—international advocacy plays a crucial role in amplifying their voices and ensuring they are included in global policy discussions.

3. Land rights and tenure security.

are critical components of sustainable development, especially for rural and indigenous communities like pastoralists. Secure land tenure refers to the legal and institutional recognition of an individual's or community’s rights to use, manage, and own land. For pastoralist communities, whose livelihoods depend on access to vast rangelands and mobility across these areas, land rights are foundational to their survival, culture, and economic activities. Advocacy efforts surrounding land rights and tenure security aim to ensure that pastoralists and other marginalized groups have recognized, legally protected rights to the land they traditionally use and depend on. This is crucial to prevent displacement, land grabbing, and conflicts with other land users, such as farmers, corporations, or governments.

4. Climate change adaptation funding.

Climate change adaptation funding is a critical issue for communities vulnerable to the impacts of climate change, such as pastoralists, farmers, and indigenous groups. Adaptation refers to efforts that help communities cope with and reduce the adverse effects of climate change. For pastoralists, whose livelihoods depend on access to natural resources like water and grazing lands, climate change poses severe threats due to increased droughts, unpredictable weather patterns, and desertification. As a result, advocacy for climate change adaptation funding focuses on securing financial resources to build resilience, support adaptation efforts, and ensure that marginalized communities can continue their way of life despite environmental changes.

5. Capacity building for local institutions.

Capacity building for local institutions is a critical aspect of policy and advocacy efforts aimed at empowering communities to manage their own development, address local challenges, and effectively implement policies related to climate change, natural resource management, social services, and more. For pastoralist communities and other marginalized groups, building the capacity of local institutions helps ensure they can sustainably manage their resources, represent their interests in policy-making processes, and address the challenges posed by environmental, social, and economic changes.

EPILOGUE:

Climate change-driven droughts in pastoralist societies significantly impact the gendered division of labor, exacerbating the workload and vulnerabilities of women. As traditional roles shift due to the environmental crisis, women bear the brunt of these changes, both in terms of economic loss and increased labor. However, there are opportunities to address these issues by promoting gender-sensitive climate policies and empowering women to play a greater role in community resilience efforts. Addressing gender inequality in pastoralist societies is crucial for building a more sustainable and equitable response to climate change.

OPPORTUNITIES FOR CHANGE:

1.Empowering women in adaptation:

Some climate adaptation programs have recognized the need to include women in decision-making and resource management. By providing women with access to education, credit, and alternative livelihood opportunities, these initiatives aim to address both climate resilience and gender inequality.

2.Policy interventions:

Governments and NGOs are beginning to recognize the gendered impacts of climate change on pastoralist communities. Initiatives such as ensuring women’s participation in climate-related decision-making, promoting gender-sensitive climate adaptation strategies, and providing targeted support for women’s economic empowerment can help mitigate the gender inequality exacerbated by climate change.

STRATEGIC GROWTH FOUND AMONG PASTORALISTS COMMUNITY

Pastoralists also adapt by strengthening social bonds through systems of reciprocity and mutual aid, such as livestock loans, communal grazing agreements, and collective action during times of crises. This social capital plays a critical role in ensuring survival when resources are scarce. Additionally, pastoralists have been increasingly integrating modern technologies such as GPS tracking for herd management, solar power for water pumps, and mobile banking to facilitate trade and financial security. Access to veterinary care, either through local services or NGOs, has improved herd health and reduced mortality rates, enhancing the sustainability of pastoralist systems. In some regions, pastoralists have adapted to governmental and NGO interventions that promote land rights, climate change adaptation programs, and alternative livelihoods, all aimed at improving their resilience to the shifting pressures of the modern world. These adaptive strategies showcase the pastoralists' ability to innovate and evolve while maintaining their deep connection to traditional practices and knowledge.
